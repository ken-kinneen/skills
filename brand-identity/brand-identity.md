# Brand Identity

A brand is not a logo. A logo is a symbol that represents a brand. The brand itself is the
complete psychological impression a person holds about a business — built from every
interaction, every visual cue, every word, and every experience. Iconic brands feel
inevitable: you can't imagine them looking any other way. That feeling is the result of
deliberate decisions rooted in psychology, not aesthetic preference.

This skill exists because AI-generated brands tend to be surface-level: a nice color palette,
a clean sans-serif, a generic icon, and no underlying strategy connecting any of it. The
result looks polished but feels hollow — interchangeable with a thousand other brands.
The goal here is to build brands where every element carries meaning and reinforces a
singular identity.

## Discovery interview

Never generate brand assets without first understanding the business. The interview is the
most important step. Run through these questions with the user before designing anything.
Adapt the language to their context, but cover all the ground:

### Business & audience

- What does the business do, in one sentence a stranger would understand?
- Who is the primary customer? Describe them: demographics, psychographics, what they
  value, what they fear.
- What problem does the business solve? What does life look like before and after?
- Who are the top 2-3 competitors? What do their brands look and feel like?
- What makes this business meaningfully different from those competitors?

### Personality & emotion

- If this brand were a person, how would you describe their personality in 3-5 adjectives?
- What emotion should someone feel within 3 seconds of encountering this brand?
- Name 2-3 brands (any industry) whose vibe you admire. What specifically do you like
  about them?
- Name 1-2 brands whose vibe you want to avoid. What specifically turns you off?
- Is this brand more premium or accessible? More serious or playful? More traditional or
  modern? More minimal or expressive?

### Practical context

- Where will this brand primarily live? (web, app, physical product, packaging, social media)
- Does any existing brand material exist? (current logo, colors, fonts, tone of voice)
- Are there any hard constraints? (industry regulations, existing brand equity to preserve,
  co-branding requirements)
- What is the brand name? If not yet decided, what are the candidates?

Use the answers to determine the brand archetype, define the identity prism, and inform
every visual and verbal decision that follows.

## Brand archetypes

Carl Jung identified universal character patterns embedded in human psychology. Margaret
Mark and Carol Pearson adapted these into 12 brand archetypes in "The Hero and the Outlaw"
(2001). Archetypes work because they tap into subconscious recognition — people instinctively
understand and connect with these personality patterns without being told what they mean.

Every brand should identify one primary archetype and optionally one secondary archetype.
The primary drives the core personality; the secondary adds nuance and differentiation.
Two brands can share an archetype and still feel completely different based on how they
express it.

The 12 archetypes, organized by core human motivation:

**Freedom & fulfillment**
- **Innocent** — Optimistic, pure, simple. Wants to do things right. Coca-Cola, Dove.
  Visual cues: soft colors, clean typography, natural imagery, warmth.
- **Explorer** — Adventurous, independent, bold. Wants freedom and discovery. Patagonia,
  Jeep, GoPro. Visual cues: earthy palettes, rugged textures, expansive imagery.
- **Sage** — Wise, knowledgeable, analytical. Wants truth and understanding. Google, BBC,
  TED. Visual cues: clean layouts, authoritative typography, restrained palettes.

**Ego & mastery**
- **Hero** — Courageous, bold, determined. Wants to prove worth through action. Nike,
  FedEx, Under Armour. Visual cues: bold typography, strong contrast, dynamic angles.
- **Magician** — Visionary, transformative, imaginative. Wants to make dreams real. Apple,
  Disney, Tesla. Visual cues: refined minimalism, surprising details, premium feel.
- **Rebel** — Disruptive, provocative, nonconformist. Wants revolution. Harley-Davidson,
  Virgin, Diesel. Visual cues: dark palettes, edgy typography, raw textures.

**Social & belonging**
- **Lover** — Passionate, sensual, intimate. Wants connection and beauty. Chanel, Godiva,
  Victoria's Secret. Visual cues: rich colors, elegant typography, luxury materials.
- **Jester** — Playful, irreverent, fun. Wants to enjoy the moment. Old Spice, M&Ms,
  Dollar Shave Club. Visual cues: bright colors, informal typography, unexpected visuals.
- **Everyman** — Relatable, humble, authentic. Wants belonging. IKEA, Target, Levi's.
  Visual cues: approachable colors, friendly typography, real-life imagery.

**Order & stability**
- **Caregiver** — Nurturing, compassionate, protective. Wants to help. Johnson & Johnson,
  UNICEF, Pampers. Visual cues: warm colors, soft shapes, gentle imagery.
- **Ruler** — Authoritative, commanding, premium. Wants control and order. Mercedes-Benz,
  Rolex, Microsoft. Visual cues: dark/premium palettes, structured layouts, serif authority.
- **Creator** — Innovative, expressive, original. Wants to build something of lasting value.
  Adobe, Lego, Crayola. Visual cues: creative palettes, distinctive typography, artful layouts.

After identifying the archetype from the discovery interview, define the brand's specific
expression of that archetype: its vocabulary, its opinions, its visual personality, and
how it differs from other brands in the same archetype.

## Kapferer's Brand Identity Prism

Jean-Noël Kapferer's prism (1986) defines brand identity through six interlocking facets.
Use it after the archetype selection to build a complete identity system. Fill in all six
facets — gaps in the prism create gaps in the brand.

**Physique** — The tangible, visible elements. Logo, colors, typography, shapes, packaging,
and any signature visual element. This is what people can point to. Ask: if someone saw only
the visual elements with no name attached, would they recognize this brand?

**Personality** — The human character traits. If this brand were a person, how would they
talk, dress, and behave? The archetype informs this, but personality goes deeper into
specific traits: is this brand witty or earnest? Calm or energetic? Formal or casual?

**Culture** — The values and principles that drive everything the brand does. This is the
"why" behind the brand. Apple's culture is innovation and challenging the status quo.
Patagonia's is environmental responsibility. Culture is not aspirational marketing copy —
it's what actually drives decisions.

**Relationship** — The type of bond the brand creates with its audience. Is it a trusted
advisor? A fun friend? A wise mentor? A bold challenger? This shapes how the brand
communicates: the Sage advises, the Jester entertains, the Hero motivates.

**Reflection** — The idealized image of the brand's target customer. This is not the
actual demographic — it's who the customer aspires to be when they choose this brand. Nike's
reflection is the athlete inside everyone. Apple's is the creative thinker. The brand
becomes a signal of identity.

**Self-image** — How the customer feels internally when using the brand. This is the private,
internal payoff. A Rolex wearer feels successful. A Patagonia wearer feels responsible. This
facet answers: "When I use this brand, I feel ___."

## Visual identity psychology

Every visual element communicates something. The goal is alignment: every shape, color, and
typeface should reinforce the same archetype and prism facets. Misalignment creates cognitive
dissonance — the brand says one thing but feels like another.

### Shape psychology

Shapes trigger subconscious emotional responses before conscious processing begins (the
Bouba-Kiki effect demonstrates this: 95-98% of people associate rounded shapes with softness
and angular shapes with sharpness, across all cultures).

**Circles & curves** — Unity, community, wholeness, warmth, protection, continuity.
No beginning or end. Feels inclusive and approachable. Best for: community-driven brands,
social platforms, wellness, lifestyle. Examples: Spotify, Target, Mastercard.

**Squares & rectangles** — Stability, reliability, order, professionalism, strength.
Right angles communicate structure and control. Best for: finance, corporate, tech,
enterprise. Examples: Microsoft, BBC, National Geographic. Softened corners add
approachability while preserving stability.

**Triangles** — Direction, energy, innovation, ambition, power. Dynamic and directional.
Upward = growth and stability. Downward = disruption. Sideways = motion. Best for:
tech, athletics, aviation, innovation. Examples: Adidas, Delta, CAT.

**Organic shapes** — Nature, authenticity, warmth, humanness. Irregular forms derived
from natural objects. Best for: food, wellness, eco-conscious, artisanal brands.

**Lines** — Horizontal = calm, stability (IBM). Vertical = strength, ambition (SoundCloud).
Diagonal = dynamism, energy (Adidas stripes). Curved = friendliness, flexibility (Amazon
smile).

### Color psychology

Color is the first brand element the brain processes. Use it to encode emotion, not
decoration. A brand's primary color should match its archetype's core emotion.

**Red** — Energy, urgency, passion, appetite. High arousal. Coca-Cola, Netflix, YouTube.
**Blue** — Trust, calm, professionalism, security. Most universally preferred. Facebook,
IBM, PayPal.
**Green** — Growth, nature, health, wealth, balance. Whole Foods, Spotify, Starbucks.
**Yellow** — Optimism, warmth, attention, caution. McDonald's, IKEA, Snapchat.
**Orange** — Creativity, enthusiasm, affordability, fun. Fanta, Nickelodeon, HubSpot.
**Purple** — Luxury, creativity, mystery, wisdom. Cadbury, Twitch, Hallmark.
**Black** — Sophistication, power, luxury, authority. Chanel, Nike, Apple.
**White** — Purity, simplicity, minimalism, space. Apple, Tesla, Muji.
**Pink** — Femininity, playfulness, romance, youth. Barbie, T-Mobile, Lyft.

Build a palette of 4-6 colors: one primary (the emotion driver), one secondary (contrast
and depth), one or two neutrals (backgrounds and text), and one accent (CTAs and highlights).
The accent should have the highest contrast against the primary — it's the action color.

### Typography psychology

Type carries personality as strongly as color. A typeface choice is a tone-of-voice decision.

**Serif fonts** (Times, Garamond, Playfair) — Tradition, authority, trustworthiness,
sophistication. Best for: editorial, luxury, legal, finance, heritage brands.

**Sans-serif fonts** (Helvetica, Inter, Grotesk) — Modern, clean, accessible, neutral.
Best for: tech, startups, contemporary brands. Risk: can feel generic without a distinctive
weight/cut.

**Slab serifs** (Rockwell, Roboto Slab) — Bold, confident, industrial. Best for:
construction, manufacturing, bold statements.

**Display/decorative fonts** — Personality-heavy. Use sparingly — for logos and hero
headlines only, never body text. Must match the archetype perfectly or it becomes noise.

**Monospace fonts** (JetBrains Mono, Fira Code) — Technical, precise, developer-adjacent.
Best for: dev tools, code-related products, technical brands.

Pair a display face with a body face. The display carries personality; the body carries
readability. Never use more than 2-3 typefaces in a brand system. Establish a clear type
scale with intentional sizing, weight, and spacing.

## Logo & icon principles

A logo is not the brand — it's the brand's most compressed expression. The best logos work
because they encode meaning in the simplest possible form.

**Simplicity.** The most iconic logos are describable in one sentence. Nike's swoosh. Apple's
bitten apple. Target's bullseye. Complexity kills memorability. If you can't draw it from
memory after seeing it twice, it's too complex.

**Meaning.** Great logos contain embedded meaning that connects to the brand's story or
purpose. FedEx's hidden arrow (speed, precision). Amazon's A-to-Z smile (everything,
delivered with joy). The meaning doesn't need to be immediately obvious — a subtle layer
of symbolism rewards attention and creates brand lore.

**Versatility.** The logo must work at 16px favicon size and on a billboard. It must work
in full color, single color, reversed on dark, and embroidered on fabric. Design for the
hardest use case first. If it doesn't work tiny and monochrome, it doesn't work.

**Distinctiveness.** The logo must be recognizable in peripheral vision, in a sea of
competitors. Run the "blur test": blur the logo to near-unrecognizable — can you still
tell what brand it is? If yes, the form is distinctive. If no, it relies too much on
detail and will be forgettable at scale.

**Timelessness.** Trends date logos faster than anything. Gradients, shadows, ultra-thin
lines, and trendy geometric assemblies that feel fresh today will feel dated in 3 years.
The logos that last decades (Nike, Apple, Chanel, Mercedes) are structurally simple and
aesthetically restrained.

**Icon-first thinking.** Design the icon (mark/symbol) before the wordmark. The icon is
what appears on app icons, favicons, social avatars, and watermarks. It needs to carry
the brand alone. The wordmark supports recognition while the icon drives it. Some brands
(Nike, Apple, Twitter/X) eventually drop the wordmark entirely because the icon is strong
enough.

## Brand voice & tone

Voice is the consistent personality in all communication. Tone is how that personality
adapts to context (a funeral director and a children's party entertainer can have the same
voice traits but very different tones).

Define voice through the archetype:
- What words does this brand use? (vocabulary)
- What words does this brand never use? (exclusions)
- How does this brand structure sentences? (short and punchy vs. flowing and exploratory)
- What is this brand's opinion posture? (authoritative, curious, provocative, supportive)
- How does this brand handle bad news or errors? (direct, empathetic, humorous, formal)

Write 3-5 voice attributes as spectrums:
- "Confident but not arrogant"
- "Playful but not childish"
- "Technical but not exclusionary"
- "Warm but not sappy"

These spectrums prevent the brand from drifting to caricature. Every piece of communication
should be testable against them.

## Brand system: making it all cohere

A brand identity is not a collection of assets — it's a system where every element
reinforces the others. The test of a brand system is whether a new piece of material
(a social post, an error page, a packaging label) can be created by someone who has
never met the founder and still feel unmistakably "on brand."

A complete brand system includes:

- **Logo suite** — Primary logo, icon mark, wordmark, lockup variations (horizontal,
  stacked), clear space rules, minimum size, and how the logo sits on light/dark/image
  backgrounds.
- **Color system** — Primary, secondary, neutral, and accent colors with hex/RGB/HSL values.
  Usage rules: which color goes where and why. Accessibility contrast ratios for text-on-
  background combinations (WCAG AA minimum).
- **Typography system** — Display, body, and utility typefaces with specific weights. Type
  scale (heading sizes, body sizes, caption sizes). Line height and letter spacing rules.
- **Iconography** — Style of icons (outlined, filled, duotone). Stroke weight, corner
  radius, and grid alignment. Icons should share visual DNA with the logo.
- **Photography/imagery direction** — What style of photos? What subjects? What mood? What
  is never shown? This is often the most neglected element and the fastest way to break a
  brand — one stock photo in the wrong style undermines everything.
- **Voice & tone guide** — The voice attributes, vocabulary, and tone spectrum from above.
  Writing examples for key contexts: headlines, error messages, email subject lines, social.
- **Spacing & layout principles** — Consistent padding, margins, and grid usage. How much
  breathing room the brand demands. Dense brands feel different from spacious brands — make
  this intentional.

## Anti-patterns: what makes brands forgettable

**No archetype, no personality.** A brand described as "professional, innovative, and
customer-focused" has said nothing. Those are generic aspirations, not personality. If
every competitor could use the same three adjectives, they're meaningless.

**Aesthetic without strategy.** A beautiful color palette and clean typography with no
connection to the business, audience, or archetype. Looks good, means nothing. Falls apart
the moment someone asks "why this blue and not that blue?"

**Logo-first thinking.** Jumping straight to logo design without defining the archetype,
prism, voice, or visual strategy. The logo is the last thing you design, not the first.
It's the compression of decisions already made — if those decisions don't exist, the logo
is arbitrary.

**Following trends.** Geometric sans-serif wordmark with a gradient icon, because that's
what every startup looks like in 2025. Trends create herds. Iconic brands look like
themselves, not like their era.

**Inconsistency across touchpoints.** A polished website with a casual Instagram, a formal
email tone, and a playful app UI. Each touchpoint teaches the audience who you are. Mixed
signals teach them you don't know.

**Overcomplication.** Too many colors, too many fonts, too many logo variations, too many
voice guidelines. Complexity kills execution. The best brand systems are simple enough to
follow from memory.

**Skipping the icon.** A wordmark-only logo with no symbol. This breaks in every context
that demands a square or circular mark: app icons, favicons, social avatars, watermarks.
Every brand needs a mark that works without text.

**Generic copy as brand voice.** "We're passionate about delivering excellence." This could
be any company in any industry. Brand voice should be specific enough that you could identify
the brand from a paragraph of copy with the name removed.

## Process: how to use this skill

1. **Run the discovery interview.** Ask every question in the discovery section. Don't skip
   the personality and emotion questions — they matter more than the business description.

2. **Identify the archetype.** Based on the interview, select a primary and optional
   secondary archetype. Explain the selection and confirm with the user.

3. **Fill in the Kapferer prism.** Define all six facets: physique, personality, culture,
   relationship, reflection, self-image. Present as a concise summary for validation.

4. **Define the visual strategy.** Based on archetype and prism: select shape language,
   primary color family, typography direction, and imagery style. Explain why each choice
   reinforces the brand's identity. Present options where meaningful.

5. **Design the logo/icon.** If generating visual assets, apply the logo principles: simplicity,
   meaning, versatility, distinctiveness, timelessness. Design the icon first, wordmark second.

6. **Define voice & tone.** Write the voice attributes as spectrums. Provide example copy in
   different contexts (headline, tagline, error message, social post) to make the voice
   tangible.

7. **Assemble the brand system.** Compile all decisions into a coherent system: colors with
   codes, type with scales, logo with usage rules, voice with examples. Present as a brand
   guide the user can reference.

8. **Audit against anti-patterns.** Before finalizing, check the system against the anti-
   patterns list. If any apply, revise.
